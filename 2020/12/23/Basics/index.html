Basics

Posted on 2020-12-23 Edited on 2021-01-02 In math tool

A collection of theorems in Logic, Number, Sequence and Function based on the first part of the course VV186 in UM-SJTU JI.

Logics.

de Morgan rules \[ \neg(A \vee B) \Leftrightarrow(\neg A) \wedge(\neg B)\\ \neg(A \wedge B) \Leftrightarrow(\neg A) \vee(\neg B) \]

Contraposition \[ (A \Rightarrow B) \Leftrightarrow(\neg B \Rightarrow \neg A) \] ​ e.g.: contraposition of quantifiers: \[ \underset{x \in M}{\exists} A(x)\Leftrightarrow \neg \underset{x \in M}{\forall}(\neg A(x)) \]

Russel Antinomy. The predicate \(P(x):x\notin x\) does not define a set \(A=\{x:P(x)\}\).

Proof:

  1. Assume \(A\in A\). Then, according to \(P(x)\), \(A\notin A\).
  2. Assume \(A\notin A\). Then, according to \(\neg P(x)\), \(A\in A\).

Functional Completeness. A collection of logical operators is called functionally complete if every compound proposition is logically equivalent to a compound proposition involving only these logical operators.

  1. \(\{\wedge,\vee,\neg\}\) is a functionally complete collection.
  2. \(\{\wedge,\neg\}\) is a functionally complete collection.
  3. \(\{|\}\) is a functionally complete collection, where \(|\) stands for NAND, namely \(A \mid B: \equiv \neg(A \wedge B)\)

Numbers.

Mathematical Induction. works by establishing two statements:

  1. \(A(n_0)\) is true,
  2. \(A(n+1)\) is true whenever \(A(n)\) is true for \(n\geq n_0\), i.e., \(\underset{n \in \mathbb{N} \atop n \geq n_{0}}{\forall}(A(n) \Rightarrow A(n+1))\)

Binomial Formula. \[ (a+b)^{n}=\sum_{k=0}^{n}\left(\begin{array}{l} n \\ k \end{array}\right) a^{k} b^{n-k} \]

Triangle Inequality. For all real numbers \(a,b\in \mathbb{R}\), we have \[ ||a|-|b|| \leq |a+b|\leq |a|+|b| \]

The Square Root Problem. There exist no number \(a\in \mathbb{Q}\) such that \(a^2=2\).

Proof.

Assume \(a=\frac{q}{p}\) such that \(a^2=(\frac{q}{p})^2=2\), where \(p,q\in \mathbb{N}\). Further assume that \(p\) and \(q\) does not have common divisor greater than 1.

Then, \[ q^2=2p^2 \] This relation means that \(q\) is even. Thus, we can make the substitute \(q=2k\).

Then, \[ 2k^2=p^2 \] This relation means that \(p\) is even. Hence, \(p\) and \(q\) have at least a divisor 2, which is contradict with our assumption.

Fundamental Theorem of Algebra. For any polynomial of degree \(n\): \(p(x)=a_0+a_1x+\cdots+a_nx^n\), there exist numbers \(\lambda_1,...,\lambda_n\in\mathbb{C}\) such that \[ p(x)=(x-\lambda_1)(x-\lambda_2)\cdots(x-\lambda_n) \]

Sequences.

General Results:

  1. A convergent sequence is bounded.
  2. A convergent sequence has precisely one limit.
  3. If \(\lim a_n=a,\lim b_n=b\), then
    1. \(\lim(a_n+b_n)=a+b\)
    2. \(\lim(a_n\cdot b_n)=a\cdot b\)
    3. \(\lim(a_n/b_n)=a/b\), if \(b\ne0\)

Squeeze Theorem. Let \((a_n),(b_n),(c_n)\) be real sequences with \(a_n<c_n<b_n\) for sufficiently large \(n\). Suppose that \(\lim a_n=\lim b_n=a\). Then, \((c_n)\) converges and \(\lim c_n=a\).

Bernoulli's Inequality. For \(x>-1\), and \(n\in\mathbb{N}\), \[ (1+x)^n>nx \]

Monotonic Subsequence. Every sequence has a monotonic subsequence.

Theorem of Bolzano-Weierstraß. Every bounded real sequence has an accumulation point.

Definition of Cauchy Sequence. A sequence \((a_n)\) in a metric space \((M,\varrho)\) is called a Cauchy sequence if \[ \underset{\varepsilon>0}{\forall} \underset{N \in \mathbb{N}}{\exists}\ \underset{m,n>N}{\forall}\ \ \varrho\left(a_{m}, a_{n}\right)<\varepsilon \] ---

Properties of Cauchy Sequence.

  1. Every Cauchy sequence in \(\mathbb{R}\) with the metric

    \[ \varrho:\mathbb{R}\times\mathbb{R}\rightarrow\mathbb{R},\ \ \ \ \ \varrho(x,y)=|x-y| \]

    is convergent.

  2. Every convergent sequence is Cauchy sequence.

Functions.

The Big-O Landau Symbol. Let \(f,\phi\) be real- or complex-valued functions defined on a subset \(\Omega\subset\mathbb{R}\) and let \(x_0\) be an accumulating point of \(\Omega\). We say that ( \(|f(x)|\) is not significantly larger than \(|\phi(x)|\), where \(x\) is near \(x_0\) ) \[ f(x)=O(\phi(x)),\ \ \ \ \text{as } x\rightarrow x_0 \] if and only if \[ \underset{C>0}{\exists}\ \underset{\varepsilon>0}{\exists}\ \underset{x\in\Omega}{\forall}\ \left|x-x_{0}\right|<\varepsilon \quad \Rightarrow \quad|f(x)| \leq C|\phi(x)| \]

The Little-O Landau Symbol. Let \(f,\phi\) be real- or complex-valued functions defined on a subset \(\Omega\subset\mathbb{R}\) and let \(x_0\) be an accumulating point of \(\Omega\). We say that ( \(|f(x)|\) is much smaller than \(|\phi(x)|\), where \(x\) is near \(x_0\) ) \[ f(x)=o(\phi(x)),\ \ \ \ \text{as } x\rightarrow x_0 \] if and only if \[ \underset{C>0}{\forall}\ \underset{\varepsilon>0}{\exists}\ \underset{x\in\Omega\backslash\{x_0\}}{\forall}\ \left|x-x_{0}\right|<\varepsilon \quad \Rightarrow \quad|f(x)| < C|\phi(x)| \]

For Landau Symbol, we have following relations, as \(x\rightarrow x_0\): \[ \begin{aligned} O(f(x))+O(g(x)) &=O(|f(x)|+|g(x)|), \\ O(f(x)) O(g(x)) &=O(f(x) g(x)), \\ O(f(x)) o(g(x)) &=o(f(x) g(x)), \\ O(O(f(x))) &=O(f(x)), \\ o(O(f(x))&=o(f(x)). \end{aligned} \]

A Useful Lemma from Continuity. Let \(\Omega\subset\mathbb{R}\) be some set, \(f:\Omega\rightarrow\mathbb{R}\) a function that is continuous at some point \(x_0\in\Omega\) and assume that \(f(x_0)>0\). Then there exists a \(\delta>0\) such that \(f(x)>0\) for all \(x\in(x_0-\delta,x+\delta)\cap\Omega\).

Zero Value Theorem. Let \(a<b\) and \(f:[a,b]\rightarrow\mathbb{R}\) be a continuous function with \(f(a)<0<f(b)\). There exists some \(x\in[a,b]\) such that \(f(x)=0\).

Bolzano Intermediate Value Theorem. Let \(a<b\) and \(f:[a,b]\rightarrow\mathbb{R}\) be a continuous function. Then for \(y\in[\min\{f(a),f(b)\},\max\{f(a),f(b)\}]\), there exists some \(x\in[a,b]\) such that \(f(x)=y\).

Boundedness of Continuous Function. Let \(a<b\) and \(f:[a,b]\rightarrow\mathbb{R}\) be a continuous function. Then there exists a \(y\in[a,b]\) such that \(f(x)<f(y)\) for all \(x\in[a,b]\).

Bijectivity. Let \(a,b\in\mathbb{R}\cup\{-\infty\}\cup\{\infty\}\) such that \(a<b\). Let \(f:[a,b]\rightarrow\mathbb{R}\) be strictly increasing and continuous. Then \[ \alpha:=\lim\limits_{x\searrow a}\geq -\infty\\ \beta:=\lim\limits_{x\nearrow b}\leq \infty\\ \] exist and \(f\) is bijective.

Monotony. Let \(I\subset\mathbb{R}\) be an interval and \(\widetilde{\Omega}\subset\mathbb{R}\) a set. If \(f:I\rightarrow\widetilde{\Omega}\) is continuous and bijective, then \(f\) is strictly monotonic on \(I\).
