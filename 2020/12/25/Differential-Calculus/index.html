Differential Calculus

Posted on 2020-12-25 In math tool

A collection of theorems in Differential Calculus in one variable based on the second part of the course VV186 in UM-SJTU JI.

Real Functions.

The Inverse Function Theorem. Let \(I\) be an open interval and let function \(f:I\rightarrow\mathbb{R}\) be differentiable and strictly monotonic. Then the inverse map \(f^{-1}=g:f(I)\rightarrow I\) exists and differentiable at all points \(y\in f(I)\) for which \(f'(g(y))\neq 0\). Furthermore, \[ g'(y)=\frac{1}{f'(g(y))} \]

Rolle's Theorem. Let \(f\) be a real function and \(a<b\in\mathbb{R}\) such that \([a,b]\in\text{dom}f\). Assume \(f\) is continuous on \([a,b]\) and differentiable on \((a,b)\) and that \(f(a)=f(b)\). Then there exists a number \(x\in(a,b)\) such that \(f(x)=0\).

Mean Value Theorem. Let \(f\) be a real function and \(a<b\in\mathbb{R}\) such that \([a,b]\in\text{dom}f\). Assume \(f\) is continuous on \([a,b]\) and differentiable on \((a,b)\). Then there exists a number \(x\in(a,b)\) such that \[ f'(x)=\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a} \]

Cauchy Mean Value Theorem. Let \(f,g\) be real functions and \([a,b]\subset\text{dom}f\ \cap\text{dom}g\). If \(f,g\) are continuous on \([a,b]\) and differentiable on \((a,b)\), then there exists a number \(x\in(a,b)\) such that \[ (f(b)-f(a))g'(x)=(g(b)-g(a))f'(x) \]

L'Hôpital's Rule. Let \(f\) and \(g\) be real functions and \(b\in\overline{\operatorname{dom} f \cap \operatorname{dom} g}\) with \[ \lim\limits_{x\searrow b}f(x)=0\ \ \ \ \text{and}\ \ \ \ \lim\limits_{x\searrow b}g(x)=0 \] Suppose that there exists a \(\delta>0\) such that \(f\) and \(g\) are defined and differentiable on interval \((b,b+\delta)\) and \(g'(x)\neq0\) for all \(x\in(b,b+\delta)\). Suppose further that the limit \(\lim\limits_{x\searrow b}f'(x)/g(x):=L\) exists. Then \[ \lim\limits_{x\searrow b}\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}=L \]

The Continuousness of Sequence of Functions. Let \([a,b]\subset\mathbb{R}\) be closed interval. Let \((f_n)\) be a sequence of continuous functions defined on \([a,b]\) such that \((f_n)\) converges to some \(f(x)\in\mathbb{R}\) as \(n\rightarrow\infty\) for every \(x\in[a,b]\). If the sequence \((f_n)\) convergences uniformly to the thereby defined function \(f:[a,b]\rightarrow\mathbb{R}\), then \(f\) is continuous.

The Completeness of \(C([a,b])\). Let \([a,b]\subset\mathbb{R}\) be a closed interval and \(C([a,b])\) be the vector space of continuous functions on \([a,b]\), endowed with the metric \[ \varrho(f,g)=\|f-g\|=\sup\limits_{x\in[a,b]}|f(x)-g(x)| \] Then every Cauchy sequence in this metric space is convergent.

Series.

Cauchy Criterion. Let \(\sum a_k\) be a series in a complete vector space \((V,\|\cdot\|)\). Then \[ \begin{align} \sum a_k\ \text{converges} & \Leftrightarrow (s_n)_{n\in\mathbb{N}}\ \text{converges, where}\ s_n=\sum\limits_{k=0}^na_k \newline & \Leftrightarrow (s_n)\ \text{is Cauchy} \newline & \Leftrightarrow \underset{\varepsilon>0}{\forall}\ \underset{N \in \mathbb{N}}{\exists}\ \underset{m>n>N}{\forall}\ \| s_{m}-s_{n} \|<\varepsilon \newline & \Leftrightarrow \underset{\varepsilon>0}{\forall}\ \underset{N \in \mathbb{N}}{\exists}\ \underset{m>n>N}{\forall}\ \|\sum\limits_{k=n+1}^{m}a_k\|<\varepsilon\ \ \cdots\cdots(1) \end{align} \] From property \((1)\) we know that

  1. If series \(\sum a_k\) converges, then the sequence \(a_k\rightarrow 0\), as \(k\rightarrow\infty\).

  2. If series \(\sum a_k\) converges, then the sequence \((A_n)\) given by \[ A_n=\sum\limits_{k=n}^\infty a_k \] converges to 0, as \(n\rightarrow\infty\).

Approximation of p-Series. For \(p>0\), \[ \frac{2^p-1}{2^p}+\frac{2}{2^p}s_n(p)<s_{2n}(p)<1+\frac{2}{2^p}s_n(p) \]

Convergence of p-Series. The p-series \(\sum\limits_{k=0}^{\infty}\frac{1}{k^p}\) converges when \(p>1\) and diverges when \(p\leq1\).

Triangle Inequality of Series. Let \((V,\|\cdot\|)\) be a complete normed vector space and \(\sum a_k\) an absolutely convergent series. Then \[ \|\sum\limits_{k=0}^\infty a_k\|\leq\sum\limits_{k=0}^\infty\|a_k\| \]

Comparison Test. Let \((a_k)\) and \((b_k)\) be real-valued sequences with \(0\leq a_k\leq b_k\) for sufficiently large \(k\). Then \[ \sum b_k\ \text{converges}\ \ \Rightarrow\ \ \sum a_k\ \text{converges} \]

Weierstraß M-Test. Let \(\Omega\subset\mathbb{R}\) and \((f_k)\) be a sequence of functions defined on \(\Omega\), \(f_k:\Omega\rightarrow\mathbb{C}\), satisfying \[ \sup\limits_{x\in\Omega}|f_k(x)|\leq M_k,\ \ \ \ k\in\mathbb{N} \] for a sequence of real numbers \((M_k)\). Suppose that \((M_k)\) converges. Then the limit \[ f(x):=\sum\limits_{k=0}^\infty f_k(x) \] exists for every \(x\in\Omega\).

Furthermore, the sequence \((F_n)\) of the partial sums \[ F_n=\sum\limits_{k=0}^n f_k(x) \] converges to limits \(f(x)\)

Root Test. Let \(\sum a_k\) be a series of non-negative real numbers.

  1. Suppose that there exists a \(q<1\) such that \[ \sqrt[k]{a_k}\leq q,\ \ \ \text{for sufficiently large }k \] Then \(\sum a_k\) converges.

  2. Suppose that \[ \sqrt[k]{a_k}\leq 1,\ \ \ \text{for sufficiently large }k \] Then \(\sum a_k\) diverges.

Root Test Using Limits. Let \(\sum a_k\) be a series of non-negative real numbers. Then \[ \varlimsup_{k\rightarrow\infty}\sqrt[k]{a_k}<1\Rightarrow \sum a_k\ \text{converges}\\ \varlimsup_{k\rightarrow\infty}\sqrt[k]{a_k}>1\Rightarrow \sum a_k\ \text{diverges}\\ \]

Ratio Test. Let be a series of positive real numbers.

  1. Suppose that there exists a \(q<1\) such that \[ \frac{a_{k+1}}{a_k}\leq q,\ \ \ \text{for sufficiently large }k \] Then \(\sum a_k\) converges.

  2. Suppose that \[ \frac{a_{k+1}}{a_k}\geq 1,\ \ \ \text{for sufficiently large }k \] Then \(\sum a_k\) diverges.

Ratio Test Using Limits. Let \(\sum a_k\) be a series of positive real numbers. Then \[ \varlimsup_{k\rightarrow\infty}\frac{a_{k+1}}{a_k}<1\Rightarrow \sum a_k\ \text{converges}\\ \varliminf_{k\rightarrow\infty}\frac{a_{k+1}}{a_k}>1\Rightarrow \sum a_k\ \text{diverges}\\ \]

Ratio Comparison Test. Let \((a_k)\) and \((b_k)\) be sequences of strictly positive real numbers. Suppose \(\sum b_k\) converges. If \[ \frac{a_{k+1}}{a_k}\leq\frac{b_{k+1}}{b_k},\ \ \ \text{for sufficiently large }k \] then \(\sum a_k\) converges.

Raabe's Test. Let \(\sum a_k\) be a series of positive real numbers. Suppose that there exists a \(p>1\) such that \[ \frac{a_{k+1}}{a_k}\leq 1-\frac{p}{k},\ \ \ \ \text{for sufficiently large }k \] Then \(\sum a_k\) converges.

Rearrangement of Absolutely Convergent Series. Assume that \(\sum a_k\) is an absolutely convergent series in a complete normed vector space. If the summands of the series are rearranged, the new series \(\sum b_j,\ b_j=a_{k(j)},\ k: \mathbb{N}\rightarrow \mathbb{N}\) bijective, converges absolutely with the same sum as \(\sum a_k\).

Rearrangement of Conditionally Convergent Series. Let \(\sum a_k\) be a conditionally convergent series of real numbers. Then for any \(\alpha \in \mathbb{R}\) there exists a rearrangement \(b_j = a_{k(j)},\ k:\mathbb{N}\rightarrow\mathbb{N}\) bijective, of \(\sum a_k\) such that \(\sum b_j=\alpha\).

Leibniz Theorem. Let \(\sum a_k\) be a complex series whose partial sum are bounded and need not converge. Let \((a_k)\) be a decreasing convergent sequence with limit zero, \(a_k\searrow 0\). Then the series \[ \sum \alpha_ka_k,\ \ \text{converges} \]

Summation by Parts (Abel transformation). For \(m>n\), \[ \sum_{k=n+1}^{m} a_{k}\left(b_{k}-b_{k-1}\right)=a_{m+1} b_{m}-a_{n+1} b_{n}-\sum_{k=n+1}^{m}\left(a_{k+1}-a_{k}\right) b_{k} \]

Convergence of Cauchy Product. Let \(\sum a_k\) and \(\sum b_k\) be absolutely convergent series. Then the Cauchy product \(\sum c_k\) given by \[ c_k:=\sum\limits_{i+j=k}a_ib_j \] converges absolutely and \(\sum c_k=(\sum a_k)(\sum b_k)\)

Radius of Convergence of Power Series. Let \(\sum a_kz^k\) be a complex power series. Then there exists a unique number \(\varrho\in[0,\infty)\cup{\infty}\) such that

  1. \(\sum a_kz^k\) is absolutely convergent at \(z_0\in\mathbb{C}\) if \(|z_0|<\varrho\).
  2. \(\sum a_kz^k\) diverges at \(z_0\in\mathbb{C}\) if \(|z_0|>\varrho\).

Hadamard's Formula. The radius of convergence is given by \[ \varrho= \begin{cases} \frac{1}{\overline{\lim } \sqrt[k]{\left|a_{k}\right|}}, & 0<\varlimsup\limits_{k \rightarrow \infty} \sqrt[k]{\left|a_{k}\right|}<\infty \newline 0, & \underset{k \rightarrow \infty}{\varlimsup} \sqrt[k]{\left|a_{k}\right|}=\infty \newline \infty, & \underset{k \rightarrow \infty}{\varlimsup} \sqrt[k]{\left|a_{k}\right|}=0 \end{cases} \]

Convergence of the Differentiation of Power Series. Let \(\sum a_kz^k\) be a complex power series with radius of convergence \(\varrho\). Then the series \(\sum ka_kz^{k-1}\) has the same radius of convergence \(\varrho\).

The Most Beautiful Theorem. \[ e^{i\pi}+1=0 \]
